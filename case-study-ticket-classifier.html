Case Study — Build + Deployment

Automating support triage without automating away the judgment calls

A real customer support ticket classifier, built to test the Human Leverage Score thinking outside of theory — where automation should fully take over, and where a human checkpoint had to stay.

The problem

Manual ticket triage — an agent reading each incoming ticket and deciding its type, urgency, and department — typically takes 1–3 minutes per ticket. At any meaningful support volume, that adds up to hours of work that's almost entirely classification, not conversation. It's also inconsistent: two agents triaging the same ticket won't always land on the same urgency or routing decision.

The approach

Rather than automating ticket handling end-to-end, I ran the workflow through the same lens as Intervyn's HLS framework: score each part of the triage process — initial classification, urgency tagging, routing, drafting a first response — separately, instead of treating "support" as one block to automate or not.

Built an end-to-end pipeline: tickets are ingested from Gmail, classified by GPT-4o with a confidence score attached to each prediction, then auto-routed by type, urgency, and department when that confidence clears a set threshold. Anything below the threshold — or anything the model itself flags as ambiguous — gets queued for a human to review and correct, and those corrections feed back into the system as a continuous-improvement loop.

Where the human stayed

Classification and routing scored low on emotional weight and reversibility — safe to automate. Anything touching a refund, an escalation, or an angry customer scored high on all four axes. That stayed human.

The handoff point is the confidence score itself, not a fixed category list. A ticket that's clearly a routine billing question routes itself. A ticket the model isn't confident about — ambiguous wording, an unusual request, anything that smells like an escalation — gets held for a human instead of forced through automatically. That's the confidence-gating doing the same job the HLS framework does on paper: routing by how much judgment a case actually needs, not by a static rule.

The results

~65%of sample tickets auto-routed above the confidence threshold (measured, small-batch test)
15–20 hrsestimated manual triage eliminated per month, at 500+ tickets/week (projected)
<10 sectime-to-route per ticket, down from minutes (measured — LLM call latency)

In small-scale testing, roughly 65% of sample tickets were classified above the confidence threshold and auto-routed without manual review. At a typical support volume of 500+ tickets/week, a similar auto-classification rate could eliminate an estimated 15–20 hours of manual triage per month, while cutting time-to-route from minutes to seconds per ticket.

The 65% and the sub-10-second routing time are measured. The hours-saved figure is a back-of-envelope projection — built off typical manual triage time (1–3 min/ticket) scaled by volume — not a measurement from a live deployment at that scale.

What it taught me

The cleanest part of HLS in practice was the confidence threshold itself — it's almost a direct implementation of "reversibility and complexity" as a single number the system checks before acting. The harder part was resisting the urge to report a bigger "at scale" number than I could defend. It's tempting to round 65% up or quote an aggressive hours-saved figure, but the honest version — measured numbers labeled as measured, projections labeled as projections — holds up better under one follow-up question than an inflated one does.
