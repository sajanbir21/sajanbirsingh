Intervyn: a risk-weighted business case for keeping a human in the loop

A workflow simulator built on an original scoring system, the Human Leverage Index — not "can AI do this," but "what does a human protect here, versus what does getting it wrong cost."

The problem

Every company adopting AI right now is making the same mistake from two directions at once: automating customer-facing conversations that needed a human, and keeping humans on tasks that were never judgment calls to begin with. Both mistakes are expensive — one in trust, the other in time.

Most "should we automate this?" decisions get made on gut feel, or worse, on whatever the AI vendor is selling that quarter. There wasn't a structured way to actually score a workflow step and get a defensible answer.

The architecture: two stages, one of them deliberately not AI

The design decision that matters most here isn't the scoring formula — it's the split between the two stages that produce it.

Stage 1 — Signal extraction (AI does this). GPT-4o reads a workflow step's description and extracts seven structured signals — things like emotional tone, intent, customer value tier, and journey stage. It doesn't make the call. It's translating unstructured text into a vocabulary the math engine can work with.

Stage 2 — Scoring (pure deterministic math, no AI). A normalizeSignals() function takes those signals and runs them through a weighted formula. Same inputs, same output, every time.

The AI can hallucinate or drift. The scoring cannot. The trust lives in the math layer, not the model.

That split is what makes the output auditable. A client can take any score and trace exactly where it came from, instead of trusting a second black box to grade the first one.

What gets scored: four variables

1. Emotional intensity — how activated is the person feeling this?

angry 1.0 → frustrated 0.85 → upset 0.80 → anxious 0.70 → disappointed 0.65 → confused 0.55 → neutral 0.20 → happy/excited 0.10

Forrester's research on emotional experience shows it's the strongest single predictor of loyalty outcomes — stronger than ease or resolution speed. A customer who feels heard during a bad moment stays longer than one who just gets the right answer from a bot. Automating a high-emotion step doesn't just fail to help — it actively makes things worse.

2. Decision impact — can this be undone if it's wrong?

legal_threat 1.0 → escalation 0.95 → churn_risk 0.90 → refund_request 0.85 → cancellation 0.80 → complaint 0.75 → billing_dispute 0.70 → general_query 0.30 → faq 0.10

If a step requires judgement — flagged during extraction — the score is floored at 0.75, because human context can't be replicated. This maps directly to Bezos's Type 1 / Type 2 decision framework: irreversible, one-way-door decisions are exactly the ones AI shouldn't own outright.

3. Relationship value — what have you invested, and what's at stake?

high-value customer 1.0 → medium 0.55 → unknown 0.35 → low 0.20 — weighted further by journey stage: retention 1.0 → post-purchase 0.80 → onboarding 0.65 → checkout 0.60 → pre-purchase 0.35

A customer in the retention phase has already bought, used, and formed an opinion — Bain's loyalty research shows a 5% drop in retention can mean a 25–95% drop in profit. The journey stage multiplies the stakes; the same complaint costs more at retention than at pre-purchase.

4. Revenue / reputation risk — what's the blast radius if AI gets it wrong?

legal 1.0 → payment 0.90 → churn 0.85 → reputation 0.85 → service_failure 0.80 → refund 0.75 → delivery 0.55 → technical 0.45 → administrative 0.20 → faq 0.05

This is the tail-risk variable. Most interactions go fine — but a bad AI response on a payment dispute or legal complaint isn't recoverable with a retry the way a wrong FAQ answer is.

The formula, the weights, and where the line gets drawn

// the formula itself — weights vary by workflow category
HLI = (Emotional Intensity × w₁) + (Decision Impact × w₂)
    + (Relationship Value × w₃) + (Revenue Risk × w₄)
CategoryEmotionalDecisionRelationshipRevenue
Support0.400.250.200.15
Sales0.250.400.200.15
HR0.300.250.300.15
Default0.350.300.200.15

Support weights emotion highest — a frustrated customer calling in is the highest churn moment. Sales weights decision impact — mistime an offer and you kill the conversion. HR weights relationship value — candidate experience shapes employer brand for years. Most "AI readiness" tools score every interaction with one fixed rubric; this one doesn't.

The output: three verdicts

HLI ≥ 0.70

Protect

Human owns this step. The math says the risk or relationship cost is too high to hand over.

HLI 0.40 – 0.69

Review

AI drafts, a human approves before it goes out. The middle ground most tools skip entirely.

HLI < 0.40

Automate

Safe to hand to AI fully — low stakes, low emotional charge, low blast radius.

Override rules — for when the math would be wrong

Before the thresholds are even checked, a set of hard-coded rules bypass the score for cases where the risk is unambiguous. A step scoring 0.68 — just under the human threshold — involving a legal threat would otherwise land in "review." The override catches it and routes it straight to human, because some combinations carry known risk that deserves a rule, not a probability.

  • Legal issue or legal threat → always human, regardless of score
  • Angry customer + refund or cancellation intent → always human (the highest churn moment in support)
  • Churn risk during the retention stage → always human
  • FAQ + neutral tone → always AI
  • Scheduling or simple acknowledgement → always AI

Confidence score

confidence = 0.5 + (distance from nearest threshold × 2)

An HLI of 0.85 sits far from both thresholds — high confidence in the verdict. An HLI of 0.42 sits right on the review/automate boundary — low confidence. This lets the system surface uncertainty honestly instead of presenting a borderline case with the same weight as an obvious one.

The evidence

The broader concept — that automation potential varies systematically by task type, not just by industry — was first stress-tested against McKinsey Global Institute's research on automation potential across occupations. The specific weight calibrations inside the scoring engine go further: each variable's scale is grounded in a named, citable source, documented directly in the scoring config rather than left as an assumption.

Emotional intensity draws on Forrester's research on emotion as a loyalty driver. Relationship value draws on Bain's loyalty-to-profit research. Decision impact borrows its logic from Jeff Bezos's Type 1 / Type 2 decision framework. The sales and HR category weights draw on Gong.io's conversion research and LinkedIn's employer-brand research, respectively. None of this is decorative — it's the audit trail for why the weights are what they are.
